Geoffrey Chaucer (; c. [CLS] [SEP] 1340s â€“ 25 October 1400) was an English poet and author. [CLS] [SEP] Widely considered the greatest English poet of the Middle Ages, he is best known for The Canterbury Tales. [CLS] [SEP] He has been called the "father of English literature", or, alternatively, the "father of English poetry". [CLS] [SEP] He was the first writer to be buried in what has since come to be called Poets' Corner, in Westminster Abbey. [CLS] [SEP] Chaucer also gained fame as a philosopher and astronomer, composing the scientific A Treatise on the Astrolabe for his 10-year-old son Lewis. [CLS] [SEP] He maintained a career in the civil service as a bureaucrat, courtier, diplomat, and member of parliament. [CLS] [SEP] Among Chaucer's many other works are The Book of the Duchess, The House of Fame, The Legend of Good Women, and Troilus and Criseyde. [CLS] [SEP] He is seen as crucial in legitimising the literary use of Middle English when the dominant literary languages in England were still French and Latin. [CLS] [SEP]
