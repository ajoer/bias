Hurricane Hiki was the third-wettest tropical cyclone on record in the United States, behind Hurricane Lane in 2018, and Hurricane Harvey in 2017. [CLS] [SEP] It was also considered the first official hurricane in the vicinity of the Hawaiian Islands. [CLS] [SEP] The fourth tropical cyclone of the 1950 Pacific hurricane season, Hiki formed as a tropical depression to the southeast of Hawaii on August 12. [CLS] [SEP] On the following day, the depression headed northwestward and intensified into Tropical Storm Hiki. [CLS] [SEP] While paralleling the Hawaiian Islands on August 16, Hiki strengthened into a hurricane. [CLS] [SEP] Around that time, the storm peaked with maximum sustained winds of 85 mph (140 km/h). [CLS] [SEP] The following day, Hiki curved southwestward on August 17. [CLS] [SEP] Two days later, the hurricane resumed moving northwestward and weakened to a tropical storm shortly thereafter. [CLS] [SEP] Around midday on August 21, Hiki weakened to a tropical depression and dissipated about six hours later. [CLS] [SEP] Although Hiki was a small tropical cyclone and bypassed Hawaii, heavy rainfall and strong winds were observed throughout the islands. [CLS] [SEP] At the Kanalohuluhulu Ranger Station on Kauai, 52 inches (1,300 mm) of precipitation was observed, which was the highest total associated with a tropical cyclone on record in the United States, until surpassed by Hurricane Harvey in 2017 which dumped a maximum of 60.58 inches (1,539 mm) in Texas. [CLS] [SEP] The Waimea River overflowed, causing more than 200 residents to flee their homes. [CLS] [SEP] Many streets and several hundred acres of sugar cane fields were flooded. [CLS] [SEP] About $200,000 (1950 USD) in damage was reported due to flooding in the city of Waimea. [CLS] [SEP] In Kekaha, the powerhouse suffered severe damage. [CLS] [SEP] Throughout the islands, winds de-roofed several homes and downed power lines; one fatality occurred after a person came in contact with a live wire. [CLS] [SEP]
