[CLS] Maurice Genevoix (French pronunciation: ​[mɔʁis ʒǝnvwa, moʁ-]; 29 November 1890 – 8 September 1980) was a French author. [SEP] [CLS] Born on 29 November 1890 at Decize, Nièvre as Maurice-Charles-Louis-Genevoix, Genevoix spent his childhood in Châteauneuf-sur-Loire. [SEP] [CLS] After attending the local school, he studied at the lycée of Orléans and the Lycée Lakanal. [SEP] [CLS] Genevoix was accepted to the Ecole Normale Supérieure, being first in his class, but was soon mobilized into World War I in 1914. [SEP] [CLS] He was quickly promoted to a lieutenant. [SEP] [CLS] He participated in the bloody battles of the Les Éparges hill as well as along the road of Tranchée de Calonne to the south east of Verdun-sur-Meuse in late 1914 and early 1915. [SEP] [CLS] On the 25 April 1915 he was severely wounded in action in his left arm and side in the Tranchée de Calonne sector and returned to Paris. [SEP] [CLS] The battle in the Meuse in which he participated, especially those at Les Éparges left a profound influence on him, and he wrote the tetralogy Ceux de 14 (The Men of 1914), which brought him recognition among the public. [SEP] [CLS] Around 1919, Genevoix contracted Spanish influenza, causing him to move back to the Loire. [SEP] [CLS] He was quite prolific during his time in the Loire area, earning a Prix Blumenthal grant from the Florence Blumenthal Foundation to support him as a professional writer. [SEP] [CLS] It was this grant that allowed him to continue with some of his most celebrated works, Rémi des Rauches and Raboliot, the latter of which earned him the Prix Goncourt. [SEP] [CLS] In 1928, his father died, and Genevoix moved to Vernelles in Loiret. [SEP] [CLS] At around this time, Genevoix started to travel abroad to Canada, Scandinavia, Mexico, and Africa. [SEP] [CLS] Canada and Africa were both admired by the writer, the latter of which he dedicated a 1949 essay to it, Afrique blanche, Afrique noire. [SEP] [CLS] He was elected to the Académie française on 24 October 1946 and was formally inducted the following year. [SEP] [CLS] In 1950, he returned to Paris and became secretary of the Académie française in 1958. [SEP] [CLS] In 1970, Genevoix, who was president of the program committee of French state radio, started a television series on French writers. [SEP] [CLS] He was also offered the Grand prix national des Lettres. [SEP] [CLS] He died on 8 September 1980. [SEP] [CLS] Genevoix presided the Friends of the Natural History Museum Paris society for ten years from 1970 to 1980. [SEP] [CLS] The Académie française literary Prix Maurice Genevoix is named for him. [SEP] [CLS]
