The 1996 Andhra Pradesh cyclone (also known as Cyclone 07B) was a small but powerful storm that left heavy damage in the Indian state of Andhra Pradesh. [CLS] [SEP] It formed on 4 November in the eastern Bay of Bengal. [CLS] [SEP] Moving westward, it quickly organized and developed a well-defined eye. [CLS] [SEP] On 6 November, the cyclone struck about 50 km (30 mi) south of Kakinada, Andhra Pradesh at peak intensity. [CLS] [SEP] The India Meteorological Department estimated peak winds of 145 km/h (90 mph), while the American-based Joint Typhoon Warning Center (JTWC) assessed peak winds of 215 km/h (130 mph). [CLS] [SEP] Soon after landfall, the cyclone weakened and dissipated by 7 November. [CLS] [SEP] Ahead of the storm, about 225,000 families evacuated, although many towns lacked proper storm shelters. [CLS] [SEP] When the cyclone made landfall, it produced strong winds up to 100 km (60 mi) inland, dropped 210 mm (8.3 in) of rainfall across a 40 km (25 mi) region, and flooded over 250 villages along a 60 km (37 mi) portion of the coast. [CLS] [SEP] About 70% of the overall damage was in East Godavari district, where two villages were entirely destroyed. [CLS] [SEP] The storm destroyed 241,802 ha (597,510 acres) of crops and killed millions of cattle and chicken. [CLS] [SEP] Across Andhra Pradesh, the storm damaged 647,554 houses, including over 10,000 that were destroyed. [CLS] [SEP] Overall damage totaled ₹21.5 billion (equivalent to ₹95 billion or US$1.3 billion in 2019), comparable to a cyclone in 1977 that also hit Andhra Pradesh. [CLS] [SEP] There were 1,077 confirmed deaths with many others missing, although many of the dead were washed into the sea and were unlikely to be found. [CLS] [SEP] After the storm, the government and local Red Cross chapters helped residents recover from the damage, while the World Bank provided money to better prepare Andhra Pradesh for future storms.
