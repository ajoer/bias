John Curtin (8 January 1885 â€“ 5 July 1945) was an Australian politician who served as the 14th Prime Minister of Australia from 1941 until his death in 1945. [CLS] [SEP] He led the country for the majority of World War II, including all but the last few weeks of the war in the Pacific. [CLS] [SEP] He was the leader of the Australian Labor Party (ALP) from 1935 to 1945, and its longest serving leader until Gough Whitlam. [CLS] [SEP] Curtin's leadership skills and personal character were acclaimed by his political contemporaries. [CLS] [SEP] He is frequently cited as one of Australia's greatest prime ministers. [CLS] [SEP] Curtin left school at the age of 13 and became involved in the labour movement in Melbourne. [CLS] [SEP] He joined the Labor Party at a young age and was also involved with the Victorian Socialist Party. [CLS] [SEP] He became state secretary of the Timberworkers' Union in 1911 and federal president in 1914. [CLS] [SEP] Curtin was a leader of the "No" campaign during the 1916 referendum on overseas conscription, and was briefly gaoled for refusing to attend a compulsory medical examination. [CLS] [SEP] He moved to Perth the following year to become the editor of the Westralian Worker, and later served as state president of the Australian Journalists' Association. [CLS] [SEP] After three previous attempts, Curtin was elected to the House of Representatives at the 1928 federal election, winning the Division of Fremantle. [CLS] [SEP] He is the only prime minister to represent a constituency in Western Australia. [CLS] [SEP] He remained loyal to the Labor government during the party split of 1931. [CLS] [SEP] He lost his seat in Labor's landslide defeat at the 1931 election, but won it back in 1934. [CLS] [SEP] The following year, Curtin was elected party leader in place of James Scullin, defeating Frank Forde by a single vote. [CLS] [SEP] The party gained seats at the 1937 and 1940 elections, with the latter resulting in a hung parliament. [CLS] [SEP] The ALP eventually formed a minority government in October 1941, when the Fadden Government lost a confidence motion. [CLS] [SEP] The Japanese attack on Pearl Harbor occurred two months after Curtin became prime minister, and Australia entered the war against Japan. [CLS] [SEP] Bombing raids on northern Australia soon followed. [CLS] [SEP] Curtin led the nation's war effort and made significant decisions about how the war was conducted. [CLS] [SEP] He placed Australian forces under the command of the American general Douglas MacArthur, with whom he formed a close relationship, and successfully negotiated the issue of overseas conscription that had split his party during World War I. [CLS] [SEP] The ALP won almost two-thirds of the seats in the House of Representatives at the 1943 election, which remains a party record. [CLS] [SEP] Curtin died in office in July 1945, after months of ill health attributed to the stresses of the war. [CLS] [SEP] Many of his post-war reconstruction plans were implemented by his successor Ben Chifley, who in 1946 led the ALP to consecutive victories for the first time. [CLS] [SEP]
