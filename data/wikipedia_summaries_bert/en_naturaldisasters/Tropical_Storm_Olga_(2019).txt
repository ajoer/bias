Tropical Storm Olga was a short-lived tropical cyclone that caused unexpected severe damage as a non-tropical system along its track across the Central United States in late October 2019. [CLS] [SEP] The storm began as a distinct tropical wave that moved off Africa on October 8 and organized into a tropical storm over the Bay of Campeche early on October 25. [CLS] [SEP] It moved north-northeast, transitioning into an extratropical cyclone within 12 hours. [CLS] [SEP] The remnants of the cyclone made landfall in central Louisiana early on October 26 and continued across the Eastern United States and into Ontario, where it dissipated on October 28. [CLS] [SEP] The severity of the storm along its track from Louisiana through Tennessee caught meteorologists by surprise. [CLS] [SEP] Intense winds inflicted severe damage, and downed numerous trees onto homes, vehicles, and roadways. [CLS] [SEP] In Louisiana, power was cut to 132,000 customers statewide. [CLS] [SEP] A peak rainfall accumulation total of 10.24 in (260 mm) was observed near Ponchatoula, with lesser amounts across the state. [CLS] [SEP] The remnants of the storm continued into Mississippi, where 772 homes were damaged or lost, 26 businesses impacted, and 27 roads damaged or destroyed; effects were particularly severe in Tupelo. [CLS] [SEP] At least 154,000 power outages were reported across the state; in the storm's wake there, a 34-year-old man was killed cleaning tree debris. [CLS] [SEP] In Tennessee, a wide swath of winds up to 96 mph (154 km/h) resulted in nearly 65,000 power outages that forced school closures for up to two weeks in several counties, and a 63-year-old man was killed by a downed tree. [CLS] [SEP] Total damage along the path of the cyclone was estimated at $400 million. [CLS] [SEP]
