Severe Tropical Storm Nock-ten, known in the Philippines as Tropical Storm Juaning, was a strong tropical storm which made a total of four landfalls in Southeast Asia, killing more than 100 people and causing damage estimated at US$126 million. [CLS] [SEP] It was the eighth named storm and the fourth severe tropical storm of the 2011 Pacific typhoon season. [CLS] [SEP] The depression formed on July 24. [CLS] [SEP] Though it was one of the deadliest tropical storms of 2011, its anticipated landfall was well-publicized and appropriate preparations were made. [CLS] [SEP] (cf.:Conson) Its peak intensity was measured at 65 knots (120 km/h; 75 mph) on the SSHS east of the Philippines. [CLS] [SEP] At initial landfall, the storm killed 75, leaving 9 missing, and causing damage around US$65.73 million. [CLS] [SEP] The storm exited land and started strengthening again as it moved into the South China Sea. [CLS] [SEP] The system turned towards China's Island province of Hainan where a yellow alert was issued and heavy to very heavy rainfall was predicted. [CLS] [SEP] Fourteen flights from Hainan's provincial capital Haikou were cancelled and rail ferry service to China was suspended. [CLS] [SEP] Some 27,700 people were evacuated from Hainan's low-lying area. [CLS] [SEP] Nock-ten claimed 2 lives and caused widespread damage worth US$58 million in Hainan. [CLS] [SEP] The system again exited land and strengthened as it curved towards Vietnam. [CLS] [SEP] On approaching land, the system and its remnants affected Laos and Thailand. [CLS] [SEP] The storm poured extremely heavy rainfall flooding the Yom River and the Nan River. [CLS] [SEP] 650,000 people were evacuated from low-lying areas as some 6,200 acres of rice and other crop fields were reported completely submerged.
