[CLS] The 1933 Trinidad hurricane was one of three North Atlantic tropical cyclones on record to produce hurricane-force winds in Venezuela. [SEP] [CLS] The second tropical storm and first hurricane of the extremely active 1933 Atlantic hurricane season, the system formed on June 24 to the east of the Lesser Antilles. [SEP] [CLS] It moved westward and attained hurricane status before striking Trinidad on June 27. [SEP] [CLS] The storm caused heavy damage on the island, estimated at around $3 million. [SEP] [CLS] The strong winds downed trees and destroyed hundreds of houses, leaving about 1,000 people homeless. [SEP] [CLS] Later, the hurricane crossed the northeastern portion of Venezuela, where power outages and damaged houses were reported. [SEP] [CLS] After entering the Caribbean Sea, the hurricane maintained a northwest trajectory. [SEP] [CLS] It passed south of Jamaica on July 1, where heavy rainfall flooded roads and railways. [SEP] [CLS] The hurricane crossed western Cuba on July 3. [SEP] [CLS] High winds on the island destroyed hundreds of houses, and the storm's rainfall damaged the tobacco crop. [SEP] [CLS] Upon entering the Gulf of Mexico, the hurricane turned to the west and attained peak winds of 110 mph (175 km/h) on July 5. [SEP] [CLS] It struck northeastern Mexico on July 8 and quickly dissipated. [SEP] [CLS] Upon its final landfall, the storm caused heavy damage in Mexico, and in southern Texas the storm ended a prolonged drought. [SEP] [CLS]
