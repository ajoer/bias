The 2007 Mozambican flood began in late December 2006 when the Cahora Bassa Dam overflowed from heavy rains on Southern Africa. [CLS] [SEP] It worsened on February 22, 2007 when the Category 4 Cyclone Favio made landfall on the central province of Inhambane; experts tracking the cyclone predicted that it would worsen flooding in the Zambezi River valley. [CLS] [SEP] The Zambezi River broke its banks, flooding the surrounding areas in Mozambique. [CLS] [SEP] The Chire and Rivubue rivers also flooded.80,600 people had been evacuated from their homes in the Tete, Manica, Sofala and Zambezia provinces by February 14. [CLS] [SEP] By February 22, the United Nations Office for the Coordination of Humanitarian Affairs reported that approximately 121,000 people had been displaced by the flooding. [CLS] [SEP] Some people refused to leave their homes and livestock. [CLS] [SEP] There were 29 confirmed casualties and a further 10 unconfirmed deaths.In early February, the Mozambican authorities did not think the flooding would be as devastating as the 2000 and 2001 floods. [CLS] [SEP] Paulo Zucula, head of Mozambique's national relief agency, said "We expect more water than we had in 2001. [CLS] [SEP] The situation is deteriorating and it will get worse but this time we are better prepared than in 2001". [CLS] [SEP] The World Food Program (WFP) estimated that up to 285,000 people may require food aid. [CLS] [SEP] Delivery of food aid by the WFP began on February 15. [CLS] [SEP] A single UN helicopter was made available for the delivery of aid to evacuation centres. [CLS] [SEP] However, thousands of people had not yet received food or drinking water, and the threat of disease outbreaks had also increased; Paulo Zucula retracted his earlier comments about readiness, saying "We were not prepared... [CLS] [SEP] it's another disaster". [CLS] [SEP]
