Satyajit Ray (Bengali pronunciation: [ˈʃɔtːodʒit ˈrai̯] (listen); 2 May 1921 – 23 April 1992) was an Indian film director, writer, and illustrator. [CLS] [SEP] He was one of the greatest filmmakers of the 20th century. [CLS] [SEP] Ray was born in Calcutta into a Bengali Kayastha family which was prominent in the field of arts and literature. [CLS] [SEP] Starting his career as a commercial artist, he was drawn into independent filmmaking after meeting French filmmaker Jean Renoir and viewing Vittorio De Sica's Italian neorealist film Bicycle Thieves (1948) during a visit to London. [CLS] [SEP] Ray directed 36 films, including feature films, documentaries and shorts. [CLS] [SEP] He was also a fiction writer, publisher, illustrator, calligrapher, music composer, graphic designer and film critic. [CLS] [SEP] He authored several short stories and novels, meant primarily for young children and teenagers. [CLS] [SEP] Feluda, the sleuth, and Professor Shonku, the scientist in his science fiction stories, are popular fictional characters created by him. [CLS] [SEP] He was awarded an honorary degree by Oxford University. [CLS] [SEP] Ray's first film, Pather Panchali (1955), won eleven international prizes, including the inaugural Best Human Document award at the 1956 Cannes Film Festival. [CLS] [SEP] This film, along with Aparajito (1956) and Apur Sansar (The World of Apu) (1959), form The Apu Trilogy. [CLS] [SEP] Ray did the scripting, casting, scoring, and editing, and designed his own credit titles and publicity material. [CLS] [SEP] Ray received many major awards in his career, including 32 Indian National Film Awards, a Golden Lion, a Golden Bear, 2 Silver Bears, a number of additional awards at international film festivals and award ceremonies, and an Academy Honorary Award in 1992. [CLS] [SEP] The Government of India honored him with the Bharat Ratna, its highest civilian award, in 1992. [CLS] [SEP] Ray had received many noticeable awards and gained a prestigious position over his life time. [CLS] [SEP]
