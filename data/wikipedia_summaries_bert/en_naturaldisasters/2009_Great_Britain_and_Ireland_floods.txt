The 2009 Great Britain and Ireland floods were a weather event that affected parts of Great Britain and Ireland throughout November and into December 2009. [CLS] [SEP] November was the wettest month across the United Kingdom since records began in 1914 and had well above average temperatures. [CLS] [SEP] The worst affected area in Great Britain was the English county of Cumbria. [CLS] [SEP] The Irish counties of Clare, Cork, Galway and Westmeath were among the worst affected areas of Ireland. [CLS] [SEP] European windstorms bringing heavy rain and gale-force winds caused damage and flooding to the south of Great Britain on 13–14 November. [CLS] [SEP] Unsettled weather continued across the south and later to the north. [CLS] [SEP] On 19–20 November, many towns and villages in Cumbria and Dumfries and Galloway were affected. [CLS] [SEP] A number of bridges collapsed, one of which led to the death of a police officer, who was standing on the bridge when it collapsed. [CLS] [SEP] Another death occurred on 21 November as a canoeist was trapped against a tree near Poundsgate, on Dartmoor in Devon. [CLS] [SEP] In Powys, there were two deaths, at Newtown and Talybont-on-Usk. [CLS] [SEP] Among the many places severely flooded was the Republic of Ireland's second largest city, Cork. [CLS] [SEP] For more than ten days, 40 per cent of its population were without running water after a treatment plant was affected by several metres of flood water. [CLS] [SEP] University College Cork was damaged and at least a week of lectures was cancelled. [CLS] [SEP] Courts were also disrupted, with some eventually being moved to a hotel. [CLS] [SEP] At the time, Taoiseach Brian Cowen described the situation in Ireland as an "ongoing emergency" that was going to get worse. [CLS] [SEP]
