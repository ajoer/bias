The 2004 Myanmar cyclone was considered the worst to strike the country since 1968. [CLS] [SEP] The second tropical cyclone of the 2004 North Indian Ocean cyclone season, it formed as a depression on May 16 in the central Bay of Bengal. [CLS] [SEP] With low wind shear and a surge in the monsoon trough, the storm intensified while meandering over open waters. [CLS] [SEP] The storm eventually began a steady northeastward motion due to a ridge to the north over India. [CLS] [SEP] While approaching land, an eye developed in the center of the storm, indicative of a strong cyclone. [CLS] [SEP] On May 19, the cyclone made landfall along northwestern Myanmar near Sittwe, with maximum sustained winds estimated at 165 km/h (105 mph) by the India Meteorological Department. [CLS] [SEP] The storm rapidly weakened over land, although its remnants spread rainfall into northern Thailand and Yunnan province in China. [CLS] [SEP] Winds from the cyclone reached 157 km/h (98 mph) in Myanmar, occurring in conjunction with heavy rainfall and a high storm surge. [CLS] [SEP] Despite the storm's ferocity, the government did not report about the cyclone for ten days, as they usually under-report on landfalling storms. [CLS] [SEP] The cyclone caused heavy damage throughout Rakhine State, destroying or heavily damaging 4,035 homes and leaving 25,000 people homeless. [CLS] [SEP] There was widespread crop damage, resulting in food shortages, and damaged roads disrupted subsequent relief efforts. [CLS] [SEP] Damage in Myanmar totaled over K621 million kyat ($99.2 million USD), making it the worst storm in the country since 1968, and there were 236 deaths, with an unofficial death toll as high as 1,000. [CLS] [SEP] Although damage was heaviest in Myanmar, the cyclone's effects also spread into neighboring Bangladesh, where strong winds knocked over trees and capsized two ships. [CLS] [SEP]
