Grand Duchess Alexandra Petrovna of Russia (Russian: Алекса́ндра Петро́вна Ольденбургская, tr. [CLS] [SEP] Alexandra Petrovna Olʹdenburgskaya; Born Duchess Alexandra Frederica Wilhelmina of Oldenburg; 2 June 1838 – 25 April 1900) was a great-granddaughter of Emperor Paul I of Russia and the wife of Grand Duke Nicholas Nikolaevich of Russia, the elder. [CLS] [SEP] Born Duchess Alexandra of Oldenburg, she was the eldest daughter of Duke Peter of Oldenburg and his wife Princess Therese of Nassau-Weilburg. [CLS] [SEP] She grew up in Russia in close proximity to the Romanovs as her father was a nephew of Tsar Nicholas I of Russia. [CLS] [SEP] Alexandra’s parents were artistically gifted and passionate philanthropists. [CLS] [SEP] They provided a good education for her and inspired in Alexandra a life of service to those in need. [CLS] [SEP] Alexandra married in 1856, Grand Duke Nicholas Nikolaevich of Russia, the third son of Tsar Nicholas I and her first cousin once removed. [CLS] [SEP] Alexandra, who had been raised in the Lutheran church, converted to the Orthodox faith, and took the name Grand Duchess Alexandra Petrovna of Russia. [CLS] [SEP] The couple had two children: Grand Duke Nicholas Nikolaevich of Russia (1856–1929), the younger, and Grand Duke Peter Nikolaevich of Russia (1864–1931). [CLS] [SEP] The marriage, arranged by the Russian Imperial family in an attempt to control the Grand Duke’s excesses, was unhappy. [CLS] [SEP] She was plain, serious and liked simplicity. [CLS] [SEP] Deeply religious and very involved in charity work, Alexandra founded a training institute for nurses in St Petersburg in 1865. [CLS] [SEP] The same year, her husband began an affair with a ballerina, forming a second family with his mistress. [CLS] [SEP] After the collapse of her marriage, Alexandra lived separated from her husband who expelled her from their household in 1879. [CLS] [SEP] A carriage accident left her almost completely paralyzed and, in November 1880, Alexandra went abroad to improve her health, compelled by her brother-in-law Tsar Alexander II. [CLS] [SEP] The following year, she asked her nephew, Tsar Alexander III, to allow her to return to Russia and she settled in Kiev. [CLS] [SEP] She recovered her mobility and, in 1889, she founded the Pokrov of Our Lady Monastery, a convent of nursing nuns with its own hospital, to provide free treatment for the poor. [CLS] [SEP] She dedicated the rest of her life to the work at the hospital. [CLS] [SEP] In 1889, she became an Orthodox nun under the name Anastasia. [CLS] [SEP] She died at the convent in 1900.
