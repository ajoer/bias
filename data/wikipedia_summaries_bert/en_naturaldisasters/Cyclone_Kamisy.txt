Cyclone Kamisy was considered the worst tropical cyclone to affect northern Madagascar since 1911. [CLS] [SEP] A tropical disturbance formed near Diego Garcia on April 3, 1984 and subsequently moved westward, intensifying into a moderate tropical storm two days later. [CLS] [SEP] Given the name Kamisy, the storm gradually intensified into an intense tropical cyclone by April 9. [CLS] [SEP] Kamisy reached winds of 170 km/h (105 mph) before making landfall in extreme northern Madagascar near Diego Suarez. [CLS] [SEP] It weakened upon entering the Mozambique Channel, but briefly re-intensified on April 10. [CLS] [SEP] That day while passing near Mayotte, the cyclone turned to the southeast, striking Madagascar again near Majunga. [CLS] [SEP] Kamisy quickly crossed the country. [CLS] [SEP] After emerging into the Indian Ocean off the east coast of Madagascar, the system reintensified into a moderate tropical storm before dissipating on April 16. [CLS] [SEP] In northern Madagascar, Kamisy produced wind gusts of 250 km/h (155 mph), which destroyed 80% of the city of Diego Suarez. [CLS] [SEP] About 39,000 people were left homeless in the area, and there were five deaths. [CLS] [SEP] In western Madagascar, the cyclone dropped 232.2 mm (9.14 in) of rainfall in 24 hours in Majunga, which damaged rice fields in the region after causing widespread river flooding. [CLS] [SEP] Additionally, the storm destroyed about 80% of Majunga. [CLS] [SEP] Throughout the country, Kamisy caused $250 million in damage and 68 deaths, with 215 people injured and 100,000 left homeless. [CLS] [SEP] Fifteen people were killed and 30 others were injured in Mahajanga. [CLS] [SEP] Following the storm, emergency food and medical supplies were rushed into the nation. [CLS] [SEP] In addition to the impact to Madagascar, one death and severe damage was reported in the Comoros Islands, where all of the banana crop was destroyed. [CLS] [SEP]
