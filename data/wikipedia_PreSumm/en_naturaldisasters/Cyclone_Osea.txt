[SEP] [CLS] Moving south and later east, it was named Osea on November 24 after achieving windspeeds equal to a Category 1 cyclone on the Australian tropical cyclone intensity scale. [SEP] [CLS] Continuing to intensify, Osea soon reached its peak intensity of 90 mph (145 km/h). [SEP] [CLS] Afterwards, Osea began to weaken because of increased wind shear, and the cyclone started moving southeast. [SEP] [CLS] By November 28, Osea was no longer a tropical cyclone. [SEP] [CLS] The cyclone brought major damage to some islands in French Polynesia. [SEP] [CLS] Around 95% of the infrastructure in Maupiti was destroyed, including 77 homes, an airport, and a town hall. [SEP] [CLS] About 30% of the infrastructure in Bora-Bora was destroyed, as well as 309 homes and many yachts. [SEP] [CLS] Many roads were also damaged. [SEP] [CLS] Almost everything on the north side of the island was destroyed. [SEP] [CLS] However, no deaths were reported. [SEP] [CLS] The name Osea was retired after this usage of the name.
