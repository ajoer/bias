[CLS] Ernest Miller Hemingway (July 21, 1899 – July 2, 1961) was an American journalist, novelist, short-story writer, and sportsman. [SEP] [CLS] His economical and understated style—which he termed the iceberg theory—had a strong influence on 20th-century fiction, while his adventurous lifestyle and his public image brought him admiration from later generations. [SEP] [CLS] Hemingway produced most of his work between the mid-1920s and the mid-1950s, and he won the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1954. [SEP] [CLS] He published seven novels, six short-story collections, and two nonfiction works. [SEP] [CLS] Three of his novels, four short-story collections, and three nonfiction works were published posthumously. [SEP] [CLS] Many of his works are considered classics of American literature. [SEP] [CLS] Hemingway was raised in Oak Park, Illinois. [SEP] [CLS] After high school, he was a reporter for a few months for The Kansas City Star before leaving for the Italian Front to enlist as an ambulance driver in World War I. [SEP] [CLS] In 1918, he was seriously wounded and returned home. [SEP] [CLS] His wartime experiences formed the basis for his novel A Farewell to Arms (1929). [SEP] [CLS] In 1921, Hemingway married Hadley Richardson, the first of four wives. [SEP] [CLS] They moved to Paris where he worked as a foreign correspondent and fell under the influence of the modernist writers and artists of the 1920s' "Lost Generation" expatriate community. [SEP] [CLS] His debut novel The Sun Also Rises was published in 1926. [SEP] [CLS] He divorced Richardson in 1927 and married Pauline Pfeiffer; they divorced after he returned from the Spanish Civil War, where he had been a journalist. [SEP] [CLS] He based For Whom the Bell Tolls (1940) on his experience there. [SEP] [CLS] Martha Gellhorn became his third wife in 1940; they separated after he met Mary Welsh in London during World War II. [SEP] [CLS] He was present with the troops as a journalist at the Normandy landings and the liberation of Paris. [SEP] [CLS] Hemingway maintained permanent residences in Key West, Florida (in the 1930s), and Cuba (in the 1940s and 1950s). [SEP] [CLS] He almost died in 1954 after plane crashes on successive days; injuries left him in pain and ill health for much of the rest of his life. [SEP] [CLS] In 1959, he bought a house in Ketchum, Idaho, where, in mid-1961, he ended his own life. [SEP] [CLS]
