Pyotr Alexeyevich Kropotkin (; Russian: Пётр Алексе́евич Кропо́ткин Russian pronunciation: [ˈpʲɵtr ɐlʲɪkˈsʲejɪvʲɪt͡ɕ krɐˈpotkʲɪn]; 9 December 1842 – 8 February 1921) was a Russian activist, writer, revolutionary, scientist, economist, sociologist, historian, essayist, researcher, political scientist, biologist, geographer and philosopher who advocated anarcho-communism. [CLS] [SEP] Born into an aristocratic land-owning family, he attended a military school and later served as an officer in Siberia, where he participated in several geological expeditions. [CLS] [SEP] He was imprisoned for his activism in 1874 and managed to escape two years later. [CLS] [SEP] He spent the next 41 years in exile in Switzerland, France (where he was imprisoned for almost four years) and England. [CLS] [SEP] While in exile, Kropotkin gave lectures and published widely on anarchism and geography. [CLS] [SEP] He returned to Russia after the Russian Revolution in 1917 but was disappointed by the Bolshevik state. [CLS] [SEP] Kropotkin was a proponent of a decentralised communist society free from central government and based on voluntary associations of self-governing communities and worker-run enterprises. [CLS] [SEP] He wrote many books, pamphlets, and articles, the most prominent being The Conquest of Bread and Fields, Factories and Workshops; and his principal scientific offering, Mutual Aid: A Factor of Evolution. [CLS] [SEP] He also contributed the article on anarchism to the Encyclopædia Britannica Eleventh Edition and left unfinished a work on anarchist ethical philosophy. [CLS] [SEP]
