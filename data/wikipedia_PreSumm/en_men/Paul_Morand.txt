[CLS] Paul Morand (March 13, 1888 – July 24, 1976) was a French author whose short stories and novellas were lauded for their style, wit and descriptive power. [SEP] [CLS] His most productive literary period was the interwar period of the 1920s and 1930s. [SEP] [CLS] He was much admired by the upper echelons of society and the artistic avant-garde who made him a cult favorite. [SEP] [CLS] He has been categorized as an early Modernist and Imagist. [SEP] [CLS] Morand was a graduate of the Paris Institute of Political Studies, preparing him for a diplomatic career, and also attended Oxford University. [SEP] [CLS] A member of the upper class and married into wealth, he held various diplomatic posts and traveled widely. [SEP] [CLS] He was typical of those in his social group who enjoyed lives of privilege and entitlement, adhering to the inevitability and desirability of class distinction. [SEP] [CLS] Morand espoused a reflexive adherence to racial, ethnic and anti-Semitic ideologies. [SEP] [CLS] His intellectual influences included the writing of Friedrich Nietzsche, Oswald Spengler, and the author of a treatise on the superiority of the white race, Joseph Arthur de Gobineau. [SEP] [CLS] During World War II, he pledged allegiance to the French Vichy regime, and became a government functionary, and Nazi collaborator. [SEP] [CLS] He served as Vichy ambassador in Romania and Switzerland during World War II. [SEP] [CLS] He was a patron and inspirational figure for the Hussards literary movement, which opposed existentialism. [SEP] [CLS] Morand made four bids for admission to the prestigious Académie française and was finally accepted in 1968, over the protest of Charles de Gaulle. [SEP] [CLS]
