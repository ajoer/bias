Tropical Storm Doria was the costliest tropical cyclone in the 1971 Atlantic hurricane season. [CLS] [SEP] The fifth tropical storm of the season, Doria developed from a tropical wave on August 20 to the east of the Lesser Antilles, and after five days without development it attained tropical storm status to the east of Florida. [CLS] [SEP] Doria turned to the north, and reached peak winds of 65 mph (105 km/h) as it was making landfall near Morehead City, North Carolina. [CLS] [SEP] It turned to the northeast, and moved through the Mid-Atlantic and New England as a tropical storm before becoming an extratropical storm over Maine on August 29. [CLS] [SEP] In North Carolina, Doria produced moderate rainfall, resulting in localized flooding and damage. [CLS] [SEP] The storm spawned a tornado near Norfolk, Virginia, damaging twelve houses and downing hundreds of trees. [CLS] [SEP] Tropical Storm Doria dropped heavy precipitation in New Jersey, peaking at 10.29 inches (261 mm) in Little Falls. [CLS] [SEP] The rainfall led to record-breaking river levels and flooding in several houses, resulting in damage to dozens of houses across the state. [CLS] [SEP] Moderate damage and rainfall continued along its path into New England and southeastern Canada. [CLS] [SEP] In all, Tropical Storm Doria caused seven deaths and $147.6 million (1971 USD, $932 million 2020 USD). [CLS] [SEP]
