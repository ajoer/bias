Günter Wilhelm Grass (German: [ˈɡʏntɐ ˈɡʁas] (listen); 16 October 1927 – 13 April 2015) was a German-Kashubian novelist, poet, playwright, illustrator, graphic artist, sculptor, and recipient of the 1999 Nobel Prize in Literature.He was born in the Free City of Danzig (now Gdańsk, Poland). [CLS] [SEP] As a teenager, he served as a drafted soldier from late 1944 in the Waffen-SS and was taken as a prisoner of war by US forces at the end of the war in May 1945. [CLS] [SEP] He was released in April 1946. [CLS] [SEP] Trained as a stonemason and sculptor, Grass began writing in the 1950s. [CLS] [SEP] In his fiction, he frequently returned to the Danzig of his childhood. [CLS] [SEP] Grass is best known for his first novel, The Tin Drum (1959), a key text in European magic realism. [CLS] [SEP] It was the first book of his Danzig Trilogy, the other two being Cat and Mouse and Dog Years. [CLS] [SEP] His works are frequently considered to have a left-wing political dimension, and Grass was an active supporter of the Social Democratic Party of Germany (SPD). [CLS] [SEP] The Tin Drum was adapted as a film of the same name, which won both the 1979 Palme d'Or and the Academy Award for Best Foreign Language Film. [CLS] [SEP] In 1999, the Swedish Academy awarded him the Nobel Prize in Literature, praising him as a writer "whose frolicsome black fables portray the forgotten face of history". [CLS] [SEP]
